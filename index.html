What is a BTC bridge?

Wrapped or bridged BTC is Bitcoin value represented on another chain, not native BTC on Bitcoin L1. A bridge, custodian, federation, vault system or signer set locks or accounts for BTC, then issues a token for a destination chain. That means "BTC" on Ethereum, Base, Solana or another network is really an issuer plus a chain plus a contract. Wrapped Bitcoin is one active model; renBTC is historical.

How bridging BTC works

Most BTC bridge flows start with native BTC or an existing wrapped BTC position, then route through the issuer, bridge or protocol that supports the destination chain. The important question is who controls redemption back to Bitcoin.

  1. Choose your BTC sourceStart with native BTC, WBTC, cbBTC, BTC.b or another supported asset, and confirm whether the route expects Bitcoin L1 funds or an already wrapped token.
  2. Pick destination chain & variantMatch the token to the chain you need: WBTC or tBTC on Ethereum and L2s, cbBTC on Base and supported networks, BTC.b on Avalanche, sBTC on Stacks, RBTC on Rootstock or L-BTC on Liquid.
  3. Lock or custody, then mintThe system may custody BTC, use a federation, coordinate a threshold signing group, rely on DLC escrow, operate collateralized vaults or accept wrapped BTC into a BTCFi protocol before issuing the destination asset.
  4. Redeem back to native BTCTo exit, follow the issuer or protocol redemption path, account for Bitcoin confirmation timing, destination-chain finality and any contract, merchant or account requirements.

Lock-and-mint vs burn-and-release

Bridge-style systems usually lock or custody BTC before minting a representation on another chain, then burn or redeem that representation before releasing BTC back. The tBTC documentation describes a threshold-network model for this kind of Bitcoin bridge flow.

Custody models compared

Wrapped Bitcoin routes differ by control model: a custodian for WBTC or cbBTC, a federation for Liquid and Rootstock-style pegs, TSS/MPC for FBTC-style custody, DLC escrow for dlcBTC/iBTC, collateralized vaults for interBTC and signer-set pegs for sBTC. Interlay is the factpack example of a collateralized vault design.

Confirmations & redemption timing

Timing depends on Bitcoin confirmations, destination-chain settlement and the issuer or protocol's redemption workflow. Avoid assuming a fixed settlement window: BTC.b documentation, for example, frames bridge movement around confirmation and redemption mechanics rather than a universal clock. See BTC.b technical specifications.

Legacy & deprecated BTC bridges

Legacy BTC wrappers can remain visible in wallets, DEX pairs and search results after their practical bridge routes have changed. Use current issuer or protocol documentation, and treat the old Ren client docs as historical context for RenBridge and RenVM.

renBTC

renBTC was the RenVM-era BTC asset, but Ren 1.0 was sunset after the Alameda/FTX collapse. Do not use RenBridge as a live BTC-to-renBTC bridge; start from the Ren client docs only for historical context.

HBTC

HBTC is a legacy Huobi/HTX-era BTC wrapper on Ethereum with weak current reserve and redemption assurances in the factpack. Treat the Huobi BTC listing as an identity reference, not a route recommendation.

imBTC

imBTC is a Tokenlon-issued ERC-777 BTC representation and should be framed as legacy, not as a current bridge path. The imBTC FAQ is useful for definition and history.

Fees, custody and security

Bridge cost and risk come from components, not a single universal quote. Compare the fee stack, custody model and redemption path before choosing a wrapped Bitcoin route.

Fees

Expect a Bitcoin miner fee, a bridge or protocol fee where applicable, destination-chain gas and any redemption or withdrawal cost. Those components can change with network conditions and with the issuer or protocol route.

Custody

Who controls the underlying BTC differs by model: BitGo/WBTC-style custodians, Coinbase account-linked custody, Binance-pegged reserves, federated sidechains, threshold signing groups, DLC escrow, signer sets and collateralized vaults. The Wrapped Bitcoin site is the main factpack source for the custodial WBTC model.

Risks

Wrapped and bridged BTC can face peg-stability pressure, bridge or contract exposure, liquidity gaps and redemption limits. BTCFi liquid-staking tokens add protocol-managed staking and reward-accounting mechanics, so review sources such as Babylon liquid staking tokens before treating them like plain BTC wrappers.

Bridging is irreversible once settled; verify chain, contract and issuer before confirming.

Bridge BTC FAQ

Does RenBridge still bridge BTC to renBTC?

No, RenBridge should be treated as historical because RenVM and renBTC are deprecated after the Ren 1.0 sunset. The old Ren client docs remain useful for understanding how the original system worked, but this page should not imply that RenBridge currently mints BTC into renBTC. Use RenBridge as a reference hub for wrapped Bitcoin options, and treat any remaining renBTC exposure with caution. Source: Ren client docs.

Is aWBTC a BTC bridge?

No, aWBTC is an Aave lending receipt for supplied WBTC, not a BTC bridge or a wrapped-BTC issuer. When WBTC is supplied into Aave, the protocol mints an aToken receipt that represents a claim on pool-supplied WBTC. That means the underlying asset is already WBTC, with its own issuer and custody model, before Aave accounting begins. For the receipt-token model, see Aave Tokenization.

What is wrapped Bitcoin?

Wrapped Bitcoin is a tokenized representation of BTC value on another chain, not native BTC on Bitcoin L1. A wrapper or bridge usually depends on a custody, peg or mint-and-burn process, so the token should be evaluated by issuer, chain, contract and redemption path. WBTC is the canonical example on Ethereum and other EVM networks, with mint and burn coordinated through its ecosystem. See WBTC for the issuer-side framing.

What happens when I bridge BTC?

A BTC bridge usually locks or custodies native BTC, then mints a representation on the destination chain. The exact mechanics depend on the route: WBTC uses custodians and merchants, tBTC uses Threshold Network operator groups, and sidechains such as Liquid or Rootstock use their own peg systems. The important check is who controls the BTC and how redemption works. For a threshold-cryptography bridge model, review Threshold tBTC docs.

How do I get back to native BTC?

You normally redeem, burn or withdraw the wrapped asset through its issuer or bridge route, then wait for processing and Bitcoin settlement. The path is asset-specific: WBTC redemption depends on the WBTC ecosystem, Liquid peg-outs involve Liquid Federation mechanics, and Rootstock uses its PowPeg sidechain model. Before sending funds, verify the token contract, destination chain and supported redemption route. For a sidechain peg example, see Liquid Technical Overview.

Why are some BTC bridge routes harder than others?

Some BTC routes are harder because liquidity, chain support, wallets or redemption access can be limited. interBTC uses collateralized vaults, DLC-based BTC variants rely on more specialized flows, and BTCFi assets can add staking or protocol-managed redemption assumptions. Deprecated or uncertain assets need extra verification before any route is considered. For the collateralized-vault model behind interBTC, see Interlay.

Are BTCFi liquid-staking tokens the same as wrapped BTC?

No, BTCFi liquid-staking tokens are a different category from plain wrapped BTC because they can add staking mechanics, protocol roles and redemption rules. Assets such as LBTC, SolvBTC, pumpBTC, uniBTC and stBTC may represent Bitcoin exposure, but their risk profile includes the staking or BTCFi system on top of the underlying BTC representation. Treat them as protocol-specific products, not generic bridge BTC. For that category context, see Babylon liquid staking tokens.
